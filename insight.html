Bridging the Digital Divide in Rural Mongolia: A Five-Year Retrospective

DT
Dolgion Tserenpuntsag
May 14, 2025
8 min read

Five years ago, FARO Foundation launched its first digital literacy program in three rural aimags in eastern Mongolia. At the time, the initiative felt like a modest experiment — a handful of trained facilitators, a borrowed projector, and a curriculum we were frankly making up as we went. What we didn't know then was how profoundly targeted, community-rooted digital training would shift not just skills, but economic trajectories.

This piece is an honest look back at what worked, what didn't, and what the evidence tells us now.

The Problem We Set Out to Solve

In 2019, internet penetration in rural Mongolian aimags stood at 38% — compared to 87% in Ulaanbaatar. More striking than the connectivity gap, though, was the skills gap. Even in communities with reliable mobile data, usage was largely passive: social media consumption, messaging apps, and video streaming. Very few residents were using digital tools to access government services, manage finances, or connect with markets beyond their immediate geography.

The standard narrative blamed infrastructure. FARO's hypothesis was different: connectivity alone wasn't the bottleneck. Confidence and literacy were. We set out to test that.

What We Built

The program ran across three phases. In the first year, we trained 18 seed facilitators — teachers, soum administrators, and community health workers — in a two-week intensive program at our Ulaanbaatar centre. We deliberately chose people with existing community trust rather than technical backgrounds.

Those facilitators then delivered structured 12-session modules in their home communities, covering online safety, government service navigation, basic financial tools, and digital communication for small businesses. Sessions ran twice a week, often in soum centres or school libraries after hours.

"I didn't think I could learn this at 54. Now I file my herdsman registration online and I helped my daughter apply for university from here."
— Participant, Dornod Aimag, 2021

By year three, we had expanded to eleven aimags and trained over 6,200 residents directly. The train-the-trainer model compounded: our 18 original facilitators had collectively trained another 34 community educators, who in turn reached communities we had never visited.

What the Data Shows

We tracked outcomes through three annual surveys, conducted independently by the Mongolian Statistical Office. The headline numbers are encouraging: 71% of program graduates reported using digital tools to access at least one government service within six months of completing the program. 44% reported increased income or reduced transaction costs attributable to digital tools — primarily through online livestock market access and digital banking.

But the most surprising finding was on confidence. Before the program, 68% of participants described themselves as "unable" or "too old" to learn digital skills. After twelve sessions, that number dropped to 11%. We believe this shift in self-perception is as consequential as any specific skill gained — it opens the door to continued self-directed learning.

Where We Fell Short

Not everything worked. Retention among participants over 60 was significantly lower than among younger cohorts, and our facilitator support structures weren't strong enough to address this. Several facilitators reported feeling isolated after their initial training with no structured peer learning or refresher support. We also underestimated the logistical challenge of reaching semi-nomadic herding communities during seasonal migrations.

And then there was content currency. Digital tools evolve fast. By 2022, several modules felt dated — references to platforms that had fallen out of use, and gaps around mobile payment systems that had become central to rural commerce. We've since built a quarterly content review into our operational calendar.

What Comes Next

The next chapter involves two strategic shifts. First, we're moving from broad digital literacy to sector-specific modules — one track for herders and agricultural producers, one for soum civil servants, one for small retail operators. The evidence suggests targeted relevance dramatically improves retention and application.

Second, we're investing in a peer network of our facilitator graduates — a structured community of practice where experienced facilitators mentor newer ones and share updated content. The goal is to make the program self-sustaining at the local level rather than dependent on FARO's continued presence.

Five years in, the core lesson is simple: digital exclusion is not primarily a technical problem. It's a human one. And it responds to human solutions — trust, patience, relevance, and the willingness to meet people where they are.

Online Safety for Youth: Lessons from Our 2024 National Campaign

BM
Bayarmaa Munkhbat
March 3, 2025
6 min read

After reaching 12,000 students across 47 schools during our 2024 national campaign, we sat down to ask the harder question: what actually changed? Not just what we delivered — but what students retained, what behaviors shifted, and what formats made the difference.

The answer surprised us, and it has reshaped how we're designing our 2025 programs.

The Campaign in Brief

Launched in February 2024 in partnership with the Ministry of Education, the campaign deployed 23 trained facilitators across public secondary schools in Ulaanbaatar and four provincial centers. Each school received a four-session module covering: identifying misinformation, recognizing online grooming and harassment, data privacy basics, and healthy screen time habits.

Sessions were co-delivered by a FARO facilitator and a school counselor — a deliberate design choice to give the content institutional ownership within each school.

The Finding That Changed Everything

When we ran our three-month follow-up assessments, the knowledge retention gap between lecture-format sessions and peer-led discussions was stark. In lecture-format sessions, average retention of key concepts dropped to 31% at three months. In schools where students led at least one session themselves, retention held at 74%.

"When I had to explain it to others, I had to really understand it myself. That's when it actually stuck."
— Grade 10 student, School No. 23, Ulaanbaatar

Peer-led formats outperformed facilitator-led ones by a ratio of roughly 3:1 on retention. This isn't a new finding in pedagogy, but it was a meaningful confirmation for our specific context and age group.

What Schools Reported

Teacher feedback was mixed in useful ways. Most appreciated the structured materials and were relieved to have professional facilitators handling sensitive topics like grooming and harassment. Several flagged that the four-session format was too compressed — students needed more time to process between sessions, particularly around cyberbullying content that touched close to personal experiences.

Three schools reported students proactively disclosing incidents of online harassment following the sessions — something counselors said had rarely happened through standard reporting channels. This was both a success and a signal that our facilitators needed better training in trauma-informed responses.

What We're Changing for 2025

The 2025 program expands to six sessions per school. The additional time is not more content — it's more space. Structured reflection exercises, peer discussion, and student-led presentations now take up roughly 40% of total session time.

We're also training a second tier of facilitators specifically for trauma-informed disclosure handling, and partnering with the National Center for Mental Health to create a direct referral pathway for students who come forward during sessions.

The campaign is also going offline. In 2025, we're piloting radio-based content in three aimags where school infrastructure limits in-person delivery. Paired with printed take-home materials, the goal is to reach students and their families together.

ESG Frameworks for Mongolian SMEs: A Practical Step-by-Step Guide

SB
Saruul Batbold
January 20, 2025
5 min read

Environmental, Social, and Governance reporting is no longer optional for Mongolian businesses seeking international partnerships, foreign investment, or government contracts. But for most small and medium enterprises, the frameworks remain dauntingly abstract. GRI, SASB, UN SDGs — the alphabet soup alone is enough to close the browser tab.

This piece cuts through the jargon. Here is what ESG actually means for a Mongolian SME, what you need to report on, and where to start.

Why ESG Has Reached Mongolia

Several converging pressures have brought ESG to the forefront for Mongolian businesses. The Development Bank of Mongolia now requires basic environmental and social disclosure for loans above MNT 500 million. Several major international buyers — particularly in the cashmere and mineral processing sectors — have introduced supplier ESG requirements as part of procurement due diligence. And Mongolia's membership in international development frameworks increasingly ties investment to sustainability performance.

The question is no longer whether to engage with ESG. It's how to do so without a dedicated sustainability team or a large compliance budget.

Step 1: Understand What You're Already Doing

Most Mongolian SMEs are already doing things that count as ESG — they just aren't documenting or framing them that way. Regular employee training is a social metric. Energy consumption tracking is an environmental one. Board meeting minutes that record governance decisions are governance documentation.

Before buying any reporting software or hiring a consultant, spend two weeks conducting an internal ESG inventory. Walk through your operations with three questions: What is our impact on the environment? How do we treat our people and our community? How are decisions made and who is accountable?

Step 2: Choose Your Framework

For most Mongolian SMEs, we recommend starting with the GRI Standards — specifically the GRI Universal Standards, which apply to all organisations regardless of size or sector. GRI is the most widely recognised framework globally, has Mongolian-language guidance available, and scales from basic to comprehensive reporting depending on your capacity.

If you operate in a specific sector — mining, textiles, food processing — supplement GRI with the relevant SASB industry standard, which provides sector-specific metrics that investors and buyers in your industry will recognise.

Step 3: Pick Three Metrics and Measure Them

The most common mistake first-time reporters make is trying to measure everything at once. Don't. Pick three metrics — one environmental, one social, one governance — and measure them rigorously for twelve months before expanding your scope.

Practical starting points: energy consumption per unit of output (E), employee turnover rate and reasons for departure (S), and number of board or management meetings with documented minutes (G). These are achievable, meaningful, and directly comparable to peer benchmarks.

The goal is not a perfect ESG score. The goal is a credible, improving trajectory over time.

Report what you measured, acknowledge what you didn't measure yet, and set specific targets for the next reporting period. That progression is the story investors and partners actually want to read.

Resources to Get Started

FARO Foundation's ESG Starter Pack for Mongolian Corporations is available for free download from our Knowledge Hub. It includes a self-assessment template, a GRI-aligned reporting framework adapted for the Mongolian business context, and a glossary of key terms in both English and Mongolian. We also run quarterly ESG capacity-building workshops in Ulaanbaatar — contact us to join the next cohort.

Digital Tools in Public Health: Five Case Studies from Mongolia's Remote Aimags

OG
Oyungerel Gantulga
November 11, 2024
7 min read

Mobile health clinics equipped with telemedicine capabilities are reaching patients in eastern Mongolia who previously travelled six hours for a specialist consultation. In Zavkhan, a community health worker now uses a tablet-based decision support tool to guide first-response care for obstetric emergencies. In Bayan-Ölgii, tuberculosis screening data is transmitted in real time to the provincial health bureau — reducing reporting lag from weeks to hours.

These are not pilot programs with special funding and exceptional circumstances. They are five ordinary interventions, running on modest budgets, that FARO Foundation documented over eighteen months. Here is what they teach us.

Case 1: Telemedicine in Dornod

The Dornod mobile clinic program connects two roving health units with a specialist team in Ulaanbaatar via a low-bandwidth video consultation platform. Since launch in March 2023, the units have conducted 1,840 consultations, of which 23% were referred for further specialist care that would previously have required the patient to travel to the capital.

The critical enabler was not the technology — it was the six-week training program for soum health workers on when and how to use the system. Technology without trained users is hardware gathering dust.

Case 2: Obstetric Emergency Support in Zavkhan

Maternal mortality in rural Mongolian aimags remains nearly four times higher than in Ulaanbaatar, largely due to delayed recognition of complications and inadequate first-response protocols. A decision support application — essentially a structured checklist with triage guidance — deployed on community health worker tablets has reduced average response time to obstetric emergencies in three target soums by 34%.

"I used to feel panicked and alone when a complication happened at night. Now I have something telling me exactly what to check and in what order."
— Community health worker, Zavkhan Aimag

Case 3–5: TB Surveillance, Mental Health Screening, Vaccination Reminders

A real-time TB case reporting system in Bayan-Ölgii reduced provincial response lag from weeks to 48 hours. A validated mental health screening tool deployed in schools and primary care settings in Arkhangai identified 312 individuals for follow-up care in its first year — approximately 60% of whom had never accessed any form of mental health support previously. And a simple SMS vaccination reminder system in Selenge reduced missed childhood appointments by 41% at a cost of approximately MNT 180 per child reached.

What Connects Them

Across all five cases, the common factor in success was not sophistication of technology — it was appropriateness. Each tool was designed for the actual connectivity, literacy, and workflow conditions of its users, not for an idealised version of those conditions. In several cases, the deciding factor was whether health workers were involved in designing the tool, not just trained to use it afterwards.

The lesson for digital health in low-resource settings is consistent: start with the human problem, not the technological solution. The technology is a means, not an end.

Advocating for Inclusive Tech Policy in Central Asia: Our 2024 Legislative Milestones

TN
Tserenpuntsag Norov
September 5, 2024
9 min read

Policy change is slow. Anyone who has spent time in legislative corridors knows this — and in Central Asia, where digital governance frameworks are still taking shape, the pace can feel glacial. Yet 2024 marked something of an inflection point for FARO Foundation's advocacy work. Across three countries — Mongolia, Kazakhstan, and Uzbekistan — we saw concrete legislative movement on issues that civil society has been raising for years. This piece is our honest account of what happened, why it matters, and what remains unfinished.

FARO's policy engagement began in earnest in 2021, when it became clear that technical programs alone could not close the gaps we were seeing. Digital literacy training was hitting real limits when the legal framework around online safety, data protection, and service access was either absent or actively harmful. We needed a seat at the table where the rules were being written.

Mongolia's First Digital Safety Bill

The most significant development of 2024 was the passage of Mongolia's Law on Digital Security and Online Protection — the first piece of legislation in the country's history to explicitly address child online safety, cyberbullying, and mandatory data breach notification for public institutions. FARO's policy team submitted formal written comments during three separate consultation rounds and provided technical input to the Parliamentary Standing Committee on Digital Development.

The bill was not everything we hoped for. An early draft included provisions for mandatory content filtering that we argued — successfully — were too broad and would disproportionately affect access to educational and civil society content. A provision requiring platforms to appoint local representatives within sixty days was weakened to a voluntary framework under pressure from technology industry lobbying. These are real losses. But the bill's core protections are now law.

"Civil society organisations like FARO brought evidence to this process that we simply did not have internally. Their submissions on child online protection standards were among the most technically rigorous we received during the entire consultation."
— Member of Parliament, Standing Committee on Digital Development, Mongolia

The UNICEF Child Online Protection Advisory

In parallel with domestic advocacy, FARO was invited to contribute to UNICEF's Central Asia regional advisory process on child online protection standards. The process, which ran from January through June 2024, brought together government representatives from six countries, UN agencies, and civil society organisations to develop a regional framework that individual governments could adapt into domestic law.

Our contribution focused on two areas where we felt the draft framework was underspecified. First, the framework's provisions on age verification were technically aspirational but practically unworkable for the majority of the region's lower-income households. We argued for a risk-based approach to age-appropriate design rather than hard verification gates, and this framing was largely adopted in the final framework document.

Second, we pushed back against language that placed the burden of digital safety primarily on parents and guardians. The final framework included stronger language around platform-level obligations and school-based education as co-equal pillars alongside family responsibility.

Engagement Strategy and What We Learned

Our advocacy approach in 2024 leaned heavily on three principles: evidence-first, relationship-maintained, and timing-aware. We learned early that submitting technically rigorous consultation documents matters less than building working relationships with committee staffers and ministry officials well before any consultation process opens. The window to influence legislation is long before the formal comment period. By the time a bill reaches public consultation, most of the hard decisions have already been made.

We invested significantly in translating our program evidence into policy language. The data from our digital literacy programs — particularly the rural survey data gathered in partnership with the Mongolian Statistical Office — was cited in parliamentary committee reports in a way that purely normative advocacy rarely achieves.

Looking toward 2025, our priorities are implementation monitoring in Mongolia, deepening our engagement with the Kazakhstan Digital Development Ministry on SME digital policy, and contributing to UNICEF's follow-up work on translating the regional framework into national action plans. None of this is fast work. But 2024 demonstrated that it is consequential work.

FARO Foundation Annual Impact Report 2024: Metrics, Milestones & What Comes Next

FF
FARO Editorial Team
August 28, 2024
12 min read

Every organisation producing an impact report faces a temptation: to lead with the wins and footnote the shortfalls. We've made a deliberate choice this year to resist that. What follows is an honest account of FARO Foundation's 2024 program year — the numbers we're proud of, the targets we missed, the partnerships that exceeded our expectations, and the strategic decisions that will define our next chapter.

The Numbers: What We Did and What It Cost

In 2024, FARO Foundation ran 38 active programs across Mongolia, Kazakhstan, and Uzbekistan. Those programs collectively reached 65,000 direct beneficiaries — individuals who participated in at least one FARO-delivered training, consultation, advocacy event, or service access session. This represents a 22% increase over our 2023 figure of 53,200 and brings our cumulative reach since 2015 to approximately 280,000 individuals.

Our total program expenditure for the year was MNT 2.3 billion (approximately USD 660,000 at average 2024 exchange rates). Of that, 74% was spent directly on program delivery. Administrative and overhead costs accounted for 14%, and fundraising and communications for the remaining 12%. Cost per beneficiary came to MNT 35,400 — down 8% from the prior year, driven primarily by efficiency gains in our train-the-trainer model.

We secured 14 new institutional partnerships in 2024, including our first formal agreements with private sector entities: a digital skills partnership with a Mongolian telecommunications company and an ESG capacity-building contract with a mining sector consortium.

Milestones That Mattered

The passage of Mongolia's Law on Digital Security and Online Protection was the single most significant external milestone of 2024. Our digital literacy programs expanded into three new aimags — Bayan-Ölgii, Khovsgol, and Gobi-Altai. We launched our first dedicated ESG capacity program for SMEs in Ulaanbaatar, enrolling 340 businesses in a six-month curriculum. Our youth cyber safety campaign reached over 18,000 students across 94 schools, with independent evaluation showing statistically significant improvements in students' ability to identify phishing attempts, evaluate source credibility, and recognise manipulative online content.

"What FARO has built is not a project — it's an institution. When I look at the cumulative reach, the policy footprint, and the depth of community trust they've earned over a decade, I see an organisation that is genuinely shaping Mongolia's digital future."
— Executive Director, FARO Foundation

Where We Fell Short

We had set a target of 75,000 direct beneficiaries for 2024. We reached 65,000. The shortfall was concentrated in two areas. First, our Kazakhstan expansion moved more slowly than planned: bureaucratic delays in registering a local entity pushed our program launch back by five months. Second, our digital health literacy pilot underperformed against its enrollment targets — post-hoc analysis suggests we misjudged the promotional channel mix in communities where health information is primarily accessed through community health workers and local radio.

We also need to be transparent about a governance matter. In mid-2024, a discrepancy was identified in expense reporting from one of our sub-grant partners. The amount involved was modest (MNT 4.2 million), the issue appears to have been administrative rather than intentional, and it was resolved through repayment and a revised financial control agreement.

Our staff retention rate in 2024 was 81% — below our target of 88%. Exit interviews identified career development pathways as the primary concern. We are responding with a new internal learning programme and a revised salary benchmarking exercise.

What 2025 Looks Like

Our strategic priorities for 2025 are organised around three themes: consolidation (deepening program quality rather than expanding geographies), institutional development (investing in our internal systems and staff development), and policy translation (monitoring the implementation of Mongolia's new digital safety law and contributing to UNICEF's Central Asia framework process).

We are grateful to the communities, partners, and funders whose trust makes this work possible. We are especially grateful for the ones who ask hard questions. That scrutiny is what keeps organisations like ours honest, and honest organisations do better work.

e-Government in Mongolia: Progress, Gaps, and the Road to Inclusive Digital Services

DT
Dolgion Tserenpuntsag
July 14, 2024
6 min read

Mongolia's e-government journey is, by most measures, a genuine success story — and also an unfinished one. The national e-government portal today hosts over 210 public services, up from just 34 when the platform launched in 2019. Citizens can renew vehicle registration, apply for social welfare transfers, obtain business licences, and access land registry records without setting foot in a government building. In Ulaanbaatar, uptake has been substantial. Outside the capital, the picture is far more complicated.

The Progress Is Real

The e-Mongolia platform consolidates services that were previously spread across dozens of disconnected ministry portals. The current unified system, despite its limitations, is meaningfully better than what preceded it. The Ministry of Digital Development reports that e-Mongolia processed 4.7 million service transactions in 2023 — a 340% increase over 2020. Satisfaction surveys among urban users are reasonably positive: 67% of Ulaanbaatar residents who used the platform in the past year rated it "somewhat" or "very" easy to use.

The Gaps Are Structural

The problem is that the third of the population for whom e-government is working is largely the third that needed it least — the urban, educated, and digitally confident. FARO's field research identified three structural barriers that consistently emerge: infrastructure (patchy mobile data coverage outside major soum centres), literacy (the platform assumes a baseline of digital experience that is not universal), and trust.

"I tried to use it for my livestock registration. The page kept loading and then gave an error. I went to the soum office instead and it took an hour but at least I know it was done. With the phone, I'm never sure."
— Herder, Arkhangai Aimag, interviewed April 2024

In our field interviews, a recurring theme among non-users was not inability but reluctance. Concerns about data security, about making irreversible errors without a human official present, and about the reliability of digital records compared to physical stamps and signatures were consistently cited. This is not irrational caution — it reflects lived experience with institutional unreliability.

What Needs to Change

Closing the e-government gap requires action on all three barriers simultaneously — and it requires acknowledging that technology alone will not solve the problem. FARO's recommendation, grounded in our program experience, is a "digital accompaniment" model at the soum level: trained community digital assistants who can help residents navigate e-government services, answer questions in person, and build the confidence that eventually leads to independent use. Versions of this have been deployed effectively in Estonia, South Korea, and Kyrgyzstan.

The platform itself also needs continued investment in accessibility — simplified pathways for the most high-volume services, offline-capable features for low-connectivity environments, and user testing that deliberately includes older rural residents. Mongolia's e-government progress is real and worth recognising. Making it equitable will require the same sustained investment applied now to the harder problem of reaching the citizens who have not yet been reached.

Cyberbullying in Mongolia: Prevalence, Impact, and What Schools Can Do Today

BM
Bayarmaa Munkhbat
May 30, 2024
5 min read

In the spring of 2024, FARO Foundation completed the largest survey of student online experiences ever conducted in Mongolia. We reached 3,200 students across 47 schools in Ulaanbaatar and four rural aimags — ages 11 to 18. The findings were sobering. Forty-one percent of respondents reported experiencing some form of online harassment in the past twelve months. Fewer than 12% said they had reported it to a teacher, parent, or school official.

What the Survey Found

The 41% harassment prevalence figure encompasses a range of experiences: repeated mocking or insulting messages (28% of all respondents), deliberate social exclusion from online group spaces (19%), non-consensual sharing of images or personal information (9%), and threats or coercion (6%). Messaging apps were the most frequent venue (67% of reported incidents), followed by social media platforms (53%) and online gaming environments (31%).

The 12% reporting rate is the finding that most demands a response. Among students who had not reported, the most common reasons were believing that adults would not be able to help (44%), fear that reporting would make the situation worse (39%), and uncertainty about whether what they had experienced counted as harassment at all (28%).

"The students who come to me are often not sure whether what happened to them was serious enough to mention. They minimise it. The ones who are most badly affected are often the ones who have been telling themselves for months that it will stop on its own."
— School counselor, Ulaanbaatar, interviewed January 2024

Why Schools Are the Right Intervention Point

Research on effective school-based cyberbullying intervention consistently points to a few core elements. First, whole-school approaches outperform targeted programs aimed only at identified victims or perpetrators. Second, teacher and counselor training is not optional. Third, the environment for reporting must be genuinely safe and genuinely useful — students won't report if they don't believe the process will help and won't expose them to retaliation.

Proven Models and FARO's Approach

Two international intervention frameworks have the strongest evidence base: the KiVa program from Finland and the Australian eSafety Commission's school program. FARO has adapted elements of both frameworks to the Mongolian context and piloted the resulting curriculum across 12 schools in 2023, training 38 school counselors and 94 classroom teachers, and delivering 8 structured sessions to over 2,400 students.

The 2024 expansion, currently underway across 30 additional schools, includes a more rigorous evaluation design in partnership with the National University of Mongolia's psychology department. We expect to have publishable outcome data by mid-2025.

Mongolia now has a credible national baseline on the prevalence of student online harassment. What it does with that data — whether it translates into sustained investment in school-based intervention, into stronger platform accountability requirements, into trained counselors in every school — is a political and institutional question as much as a technical one.
